Frequently asked questions

What researchers and research administrators most often want to know about the forthcoming prohibition.

The basics

What is this policy, in one sentence?

NSF plans to prohibit NSF funds from being used for collaboration with entities on specified U.S. restricted party lists — and to bar senior/key personnel on NSF awards from collaborating with, holding positions with, or receiving research support from those entities.

When does it take effect?

NSF intends to implement it in Fiscal Year 2027, which begins October 1, 2026. The effective date is tied to the new Draft Guidance on Financial Assistance (the successor to the Proposal & Award Policies & Procedures Guide) or the effective date of Acquisition Policy issued by the Senior Procurement Executive. The July 8, 2026 DCL is advance notice, not the trigger — nothing is prohibited by the DCL itself.

What is a "restricted entity"?

An entity on any of the U.S. restricted party lists enumerated in Appendix A of the DCL: the Department of War's Sec. 1260H and Sec. 1286 lists; Commerce/BIS's Entity List, Military End-User List, and Denied Persons List; Treasury/OFAC's E.O. 14032 Annex and NS-CMIC List; State's Debarred Parties and Nonproliferation Sanctions; the FCC's Covered List; DHS's UFLPA Entity List; and CBP's Withhold Release Orders & Findings. See the list explainers for what each covers.

Who counts as "senior/key personnel"?

The DCL uses NSF's standard term. Under current NSF guidance, senior/key personnel are the PI, co-PIs, and other named individuals who contribute in a substantive, meaningful way to project execution — the same people who file Biographical Sketches and Current & Pending (Other) Support. Watch the FY2027 guidance for the operative definition.

Scope — what's actually prohibited

What exactly will senior/key personnel be prohibited from doing?

Three things, for the duration of the NSF award:

  • Collaborating with a restricted entity — or an employee of one — on research funded by their NSF award;
  • Holding an appointment or position with a restricted entity;
  • Receiving research support from a restricted entity.

Separately, NSF funds themselves may not flow to any organization for the purpose of research collaboration with restricted entities.

Does the prohibition cover employees of restricted entities, or just the entities?

Both. The DCL is explicit that the funding prohibition "also extends to the employees of such restricted entities," and that senior/key personnel may not collaborate with "a restricted entity, or employee thereof," on NSF-funded research. So a collaboration with an individual researcher employed by a listed university or company is covered even if the entity itself isn't formally a party to the project.

What counts as "collaboration"? Does co-authorship count?

The DCL doesn't define it. The prohibition applies to collaboration "on research funded by their NSF award," which most naturally covers substantive joint research activity on the funded project. Whether specific activities — co-authorship arising from prior work, data sharing, use of shared instruments, conference co-organizing — fall inside will need to be settled by the FY2027 guidance. Flag gray areas to your research security office and NSF now rather than guessing later.

Does this affect work that isn't funded by NSF?

Partly. The collaboration prohibition is scoped to research funded by the NSF award. But the appointment/position and research support prohibitions attach to the person: a senior/key person on an NSF award may not hold a position with, or receive research support from, a restricted entity during the award — regardless of which project that position or support relates to.

What about existing awards made before FY2027?

The DCL doesn't say. It describes certification at proposal submission, which points to new proposals — but how the rules will apply to active awards, renewals, and supplements is an open question until the guidance issues. This is one of the most consequential unknowns; raise it with researchsecurity@nsf.gov if it affects you.

An entity I work with was just added to a list mid-award. What then?

The DCL doesn't address mid-award list changes. Institutions are responsible for "identifying and addressing prohibited activities before NSF funds are expended," which implies ongoing screening rather than a one-time check. Until NSF clarifies, the prudent posture is: monitor the lists (several update monthly), and treat a new listing as an immediate compliance question for your research security office.

Certifications and screening

What will I have to certify, and who certifies?

Once the policy is effective, both the organization's Authorized Organizational Representative (AOR) and senior/key personnel will certify compliance with the prohibition at proposal submission. Exact certification language hasn't been published yet. False certifications to a federal agency carry serious consequences, so institutions should build screening into the proposal workflow rather than treating the certification as a formality.

How do I actually check whether someone is on these lists?
  1. Search this site for a fast first pass across a snapshot of all 13 lists at once.
  2. Screen the official Consolidated Screening List, which covers several (not all) of the lists with fuzzy-matching support.
  3. Check the lists the CSL doesn't cover at their official sources — the 1260H and 1286 lists, FCC Covered List, UFLPA Entity List, and CBP WROs. Links on the Lists page.
  4. For any hit or near-miss, involve your institution's research security or export control office before concluding anything.

Screen aliases and parent/subsidiary relationships, not just exact names — entities appear under translated and abbreviated names.

My institution has no research security office. Who can help?

Start with your sponsored programs office (your AOR will be certifying, so they have direct stakes). Export control officers, where they exist, know these lists well — most predate this policy as export-control and sanctions tools. NSF itself answers policy questions at researchsecurity@nsf.gov, and NSF's research security page collects related requirements and training resources.

Context

Why is NSF doing this?

Per the DCL: the U.S. government has determined that these entities' behavior warrants restrictions based on national security and other foreign-policy interests, and NSF concluded that research-security risk mitigation for projects involving them "is not sufficient" — hence a prohibition rather than case-by-case mitigation. NSF also cites alignment with the Department of War's approach to restricted-party collaborations, aiming for consistency across agencies and lower administrative burden.

How does this relate to other research security requirements?

It's additive. Existing requirements — disclosure of current and pending support, the prohibition on malign foreign talent recruitment program participation (CHIPS and Science Act Sec. 10632), research security training — all continue. This policy adds a categorical bar on a defined set of entities. Note the overlap is imperfect: an entity can be absent from every Appendix A list and still create disclosure obligations, and vice versa.

Is this site affiliated with NSF?

No. This is an unofficial, community-built guide. It exists because checking 13 lists across 7 agencies is genuinely tedious, and researchers deserve a fast way to orient themselves. The official word is always the DCL, the forthcoming guidance, and the list-owning agencies. See About & Data for how this site is built and its limitations.
