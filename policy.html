The policy, explained

What NSF's July 8, 2026 Dear Colleague Letter says, who it affects, and when it takes effect — in plain language, with the original text alongside.

The one-paragraph version

NSF has given the research community advance notice that, starting in Fiscal Year 2027, it plans to prohibit the use of NSF funds for collaboration with "restricted entities" — organizations that appear on any of the U.S. restricted party lists enumerated in the DCL's Appendix A (maintained by the Departments of War, Commerce, Treasury, State, and Homeland Security, the FCC, and CBP). The prohibition reaches both the entities themselves and their employees, and it also bars senior/key personnel on NSF awards from holding appointments with, or receiving research support from, restricted entities. Compliance will be certified at proposal submission by both the institution's Authorized Organizational Representative (AOR) and senior/key personnel.

Why NSF says it's doing this: the U.S. government has determined these entities' behavior warrants restrictions on national-security and foreign-policy grounds, and NSF concluded that ordinary research-security risk mitigation is not sufficient for projects involving them. NSF also notes the approach deliberately aligns with the Department of War's treatment of restricted-party collaborations, to promote consistency and reduce administrative burden across agencies.

Key dates

July 8, 2026

Dear Colleague Letter issued — advance notice so institutions and researchers have time to prepare.

FY2027 (begins Oct 1, 2026)

NSF intends to implement the prohibition, tied to the effective date of the new Draft Guidance on Financial Assistance (successor to the PAPPG) or of Acquisition Policy issued by the Senior Procurement Executive.

At proposal submission

Once effective: AORs and senior/key personnel certify compliance with the prohibition as part of submitting each proposal.

What exactly will be prohibited

The DCL previews two rules NSF plans to add to its guidance:

1. No NSF funding for collaborative research with restricted entities

"NSF appropriated funds shall not be used for grants, contracts, other transactions or other assistance to an organization if the purpose is to conduct research in collaboration with restricted entities. This funding prohibition also extends to the employees of such restricted entities."

In practice: an NSF-funded project cannot have a restricted entity — or a person employed by one — as a research collaborator. This applies at the level of what the funds are for, and it covers grants, contracts, and other transactions alike.

2. Personal restrictions on senior/key personnel

"Senior/key personnel are prohibited from holding an appointment or position with, or receiving research support from, a restricted entity. Further, senior/key personnel are prohibited from collaborating with a restricted entity, or employee thereof, on research funded by their NSF award."

Senior/key personnel on an NSF award face three distinct prohibitions for the duration of the award:

  • No appointments or positions with a restricted entity (e.g., honorary, adjunct, or visiting appointments);
  • No research support received from a restricted entity (e.g., funding, personnel, or in-kind resources); and
  • No collaboration with a restricted entity or its employees on the research funded by their NSF award.

Who is responsible for what

RoleResponsibility under the forthcoming policy
Senior/key personnel Certify compliance at proposal submission; avoid prohibited collaborations, appointments/positions, and research support for the duration of the award.
Authorized Organizational Representatives (AORs) Certify on behalf of the organization at proposal submission.
Organizations / institutions Ensure senior/key personnel are aware of and comply with the prohibition; identify and address prohibited activities before NSF funds are expended.

What counts as a "restricted entity"

Appendix A of the DCL enumerates the U.S. restricted party lists that define the policy's scope — spanning seven federal agencies. This site maintains a searchable snapshot of each and an explainer for each list with links to the authoritative versions:

  • Department of War: Sec. 1260H NDAA FY2021 list (Chinese military companies); Sec. 1286 NDAA FY2019 list (academic/research institutions)
  • Commerce (BIS): Entity List; Military End-User List; Denied Persons List
  • Treasury (OFAC): Annex of Executive Order 14032; Non-SDN Chinese Military-Industrial Complex Companies List
  • State: Debarred Parties (statutory and administrative); Nonproliferation Sanctions
  • FCC: Covered List (Secure Networks Act Sec. 2)
  • DHS: UFLPA Entity List
  • CBP: Withhold Release Orders & Findings

What the DCL does not yet answer

The DCL is advance notice; the operative details arrive with the FY2027 guidance. Questions we suggest tracking (and raising with NSF or your institution):

  • How the prohibition will apply to existing awards versus new proposals;
  • Precisely how "collaboration" will be defined (e.g., co-authorship, data sharing, shared facilities);
  • How list changes mid-award will be handled (an entity added after an award begins);
  • The exact certification language and any exception or waiver process.

NSF directs questions to researchsecurity@nsf.gov. The successor to the PAPPG — the Draft Guidance on Financial Assistance — can be tracked on regulations.gov (docket NSF-2026-OTR-0001).
